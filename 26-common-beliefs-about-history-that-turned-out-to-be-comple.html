26 Common Beliefs About History That Turned Out To Be Completely False

United States · 2026-06-21

History is often taught as a collection of facts, but the truth is, many of the most popular historical beliefs are completely false. From Hollywood blockbusters to classroom lessons, certain myths have been repeated so often that they’ve become accepted as reality. The pyramids weren’t built by slaves, but by skilled workers paid in tax exemptions and prime beef. And Julius Caesar never said “Et tu, Brute?” — that was Shakespeare. Let’s debunk some common misconceptions about history that turned out to be completely false.

The Myth of Slave Labor and Ancient Rome

One of the most enduring common beliefs about history is that Egyptian pyramids were constructed by thousands of enslaved people. However, archaeological evidence from the tombs of the workers reveals that they were actually well-fed, skilled laborers who received payment in the form of tax exemptions and prime beef. Similarly, the famous Roman salute often depicted in paintings and movies is a 1784 invention that the Nazis borrowed; Romans never used it. Julius Caesar’s last words, “Et tu, Brute?” are pure fiction from Shakespeare’s play, not historical fact. Every movie you’ve ever seen about ancient Rome? Mostly wrong.

Medieval and Modern Misconceptions

Another false belief is that people in the Middle Ages thought the Earth was flat. This myth was popularized in the 19th century to discredit medieval culture; most educated Europeans knew the world was round long before Columbus. Speaking of Columbus, he never actually set foot on North America. The Vikings landed there centuries earlier. And let’s not forget that such historical myths about history sometimes distort the truth about famous events. For example, the idea that Marie Antoinette said “Let them eat cake” is also false—it was attributed to her decades after her death.

Why These Myths Persist

Common false beliefs about history stick around because they simplify complex events into memorable stories. Popular culture reinforces these misconceptions through movies, books, and even school curriculums. The real history is often more nuanced and interesting than the legend. For instance, the myth that Napoleon was short came from a mistranslation of French measurements, and the idea that witches were burned at the stake in Salem is wrong—they were hanged. These examples highlight how debunking historical myths can reveal the true, often more fascinating stories behind our past.

In conclusion, questioning what we think we know about history can be eye-opening. The 26 common beliefs that turned out to be completely false remind us that history is a living subject, constantly updated by new evidence. Next time you hear a tale about ancient wonders or famous figures, remember: the most popular history is often the most fictional.
