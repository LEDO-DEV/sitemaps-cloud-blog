Sources: Bucks trading Giannis to Heat for huge ha...

United States · 2026-06-23

It’s the kind of trade rumor that stops NBA Twitter dead in its tracks: Giannis Antetokounmpo to the Miami Heat. According to sources, the proposed deal would send the two-time MVP and champion to South Beach in exchange for Tyler Herro, Jaime Jaquez Jr., Kel'el Ware, and the 13th overall pick. Reaction was immediate, with one fan posting: “That’s the package for a two-time MVP? Milwaukee just traded a decade of dominance for three guys on expiring deals and a mid-first rounder. The Heat fleeced them and nobody wants to say it.” But is that assessment fair, or is it overlooking the brutal realities of modern NBA roster building?

The Reality of a Giannis Trade Package

Let’s be honest. In a vacuum, trading a generational talent like Giannis for a package centered around Tyler Herro, two unproven young players, and the 13th pick feels underwhelming. It’s easy to look at that haul and think Miami pulled off a heist. However, the NBA trade market for superstars rarely returns equal value—especially when the player might have input on their destination. Milwaukee, if forced into a rebuild, isn’t looking for a superstar in return; they’re looking for a combination of cap flexibility, young assets, and draft capital. Herro is a proven 20-point scorer. Jaquez showed flashes as a rookie. Ware has lottery-pick upside. The 13th pick can be a valuable trade chip. The “three guys on expiring deals” criticism ignores that expiring contracts are leverage—they let the Bucks reshape their future salary sheet.

Why the Heat Offer Might Be the Best Available

The deeper truth is that no team can offer “fair” value for Giannis. You don’t replace a unicorn. The Heat’s offer might actually be the most competitive because it gives Milwaukee a young core to build around while avoiding the long-term albatross contracts that often come in superstar trades. Other contenders might offer draft picks, but picks are gambles. Herro, Jaquez, and Ware are known commodities with upside. The “Heat fleeced them” narrative is emotional, not analytical. Milwaukee would be choosing a reboot over a slow decline. And for a small market team, that choice is often the smartest play.
