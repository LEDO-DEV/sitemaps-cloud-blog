Polymarket reportedly paid creators to post deceptive videos about fake bets

United States · 2026-06-21

Polymarket Reportedly Paid Creators to Post Deceptive Videos About Fake Bets

When a prediction market platform like Polymarket pays creators to film fake winning bets on clone websites and instructs them not to disclose the payments, trust takes a serious hit. A recent Wall Street Journal investigation uncovered over 1,100 deceptive videos pushed by a marketing army, raising questions about the integrity of the platform. One college student even defended the scheme by comparing it to fast food commercials, claiming, “We’re depicting what actually happens.” This comparison, however, feels more like a convenient justification than a legitimate parallel, especially when the videos involve fake bets on clone sites that mimic reality without transparency.

The Mechanics of the Deceptive Campaign

The deceptive videos reportedly showed creators celebrating wins on clone websites designed to look like Polymarket. These creators were paid to post the content without disclosing the payments, misleading viewers into believing the outcomes were genuine. The strategy leveraged social media algorithms to amplify the reach of these fake betting scenarios, potentially influencing user behavior and trust in the platform. While Polymarket now promises an audit, the damage to its credibility may already be done. The platform’s reliance on transparency for success makes these revelations particularly damaging, as they undermine the very foundation of a prediction market.

The Fast Food Commercial Defense and Its Flaws

The college student’s defense, comparing the fake bets to fast food commercials, overlooks a critical distinction: fast food ads don’t explicitly claim to depict a real transaction. In this case, the videos presented a fabricated reality without disclosure, which could mislead users into making real financial decisions based on false premises. Polymarket’s platform deals in real money and real predictions, where trust in the accuracy of information is paramount. The comparison to fast food fails because the stakes—financial and reputational—are far higher. The platform’s promise of an audit now seems like a reactive measure rather than a proactive commitment to integrity.

In conclusion, the Polymarket deception, uncovered by the WSJ, highlights how easily trust can be eroded when transparency is compromised. The platform’s marketing army pushed over 1,100 videos, creating an illusion of success that may have misled real users. As Polymarket vows to audit its practices, the episode serves as a cautionary tale for any platform relying on user-generated content and real money transactions. Rebuilding trust will require more than just an audit—it will demand a fundamental commitment to honesty and disclosure.
