It's (finally) OK to dream big about the USMNT at ...

United States · 2026-06-20

It’s been a long time coming, but it’s finally OK to dream big about the USMNT at the World Cup. After years of cautious optimism and painful near-misses, the narrative has shifted. When former USMNT standout turned pundit Richards said winning the World Cup is "not ridiculous," it wasn't just hot air. He's right, and the receipts back it up. The USMNT just beat Australia 2-0 without Christian Pulisic, their talismanic captain, and the performance was anything but a fluke. They’ve clinched Group D with three wins and three clean sheets. That’s not just good; it’s historic.

A Defensive Fortress Unlike Any Other

Let’s talk numbers. No team has ever won the World Cup with zero goals conceded in the group stage — but this squad has a chance to be the first. That’s not a hopeful guess; it’s a statistical possibility grounded in reality. The defense is stonewall. Against Australia, they absorbed pressure, snuffed out counterattacks, and made every tackle count. Freeman was a surprise star, stepping up in Pulisic’s absence to not only hold the line but push forward with purpose. This isn’t the same USMNT that struggled to break down bunkered defenses in past tournaments. They’re composed, disciplined, and ruthless when it counts.

Pochettino Finally Has Them Believing

The biggest change? The mentality. Manager Mauricio Pochettino finally has them believing. Under his guidance, the team has shed its inferiority complex. They no longer play not to lose; they play to dominate. The 2-0 win over Australia was a masterclass in game management: score early, control possession, and kill the game off without drama. Pochettino’s tactical adjustments have turned a talented but erratic group into a cohesive unit that trusts the system. The players speak with confidence, not arrogance, and their on-field chemistry reflects that. For a program that once feared the big stage, the USMNT now looks like it belongs there.

So, tell me I’m wrong. The dream of lifting the World Cup trophy isn’t just a fantasy anymore. It’s a legitimate goal backed by results, a rock-solid defense, and a coach who knows how to win. For the first time in a generation, it’s OK to believe.
