Hungarian Composer Mihály Víg Reflects on Four Decades of Scoring Béla Tarr’s Films at Shanghai Masterclass

United States · 2026-06-20

The Music That Built Béla Tarr’s World

For decades, the mesmerizing power of Béla Tarr’s films has been attributed to his unblinking camera, his black-and-white landscapes, and his radically slow pacing. But at a recent Shanghai masterclass, Hungarian composer Mihály Víg offered a crucial counterpoint. While audiences and critics praise Tarr’s vision, few recognize the sonic architecture that holds it all together. Víg’s revelation? The music wasn’t an afterthought—it was the blueprint.

How Víg Composed Before the Camera Rolled

Víg’s method shatters conventional film scoring. For *The Turin Horse*, he recorded every instrument himself—cello, violin, piano—each track in isolation. With only four or five takes allowed, the performances were raw, fragile, and unforgiving. The piano for *Werckmeister Harmonies* was captured in a single take. This wasn’t improvisation; it was precision under pressure. Tarr didn’t add music to completed scenes. Instead, he shot to Víg’s pre-recorded compositions. The score dictated the rhythm, the duration, and the emotional arc of each shot. In this workflow, the composer wasn’t supporting the director—he was setting the foundation.

The Auteur Behind the Keys

Tarr himself acknowledged this relationship when casting Víg as the accordion-playing doctor in *Sátántangó*. “You can only show what’s already there,” Tarr said, implying that Víg’s musical presence was more than thematic—it was structural. This challenges the standard auteur narrative. If the score came *before* the film, and if Tarr built sequences around Víg’s repeating, minimalist motifs, then who truly authored those scenes? It’s a provocative idea: the real auteur of these cinematic masterpieces might have been behind the keys, not the camera. Mihály Víg’s career spans four decades, yet his role in shaping Tarr’s universe remains underexplored.

Conclusion

Víg’s Shanghai masterclass didn’t just celebrate his music; it asked us to reconsider how film art is made. While the world fixates on the director’s eye, Víg’s old piano and solitary cello remind us that some of cinema’s greatest visions are actually heard first.
