🥶 WNBA's 15 most clutch players of all time

United States · 2026-06-26

The debate over the WNBA’s most clutch players is a firestarter among fans, and this latest ranking—putting A’ja Wilson at No. 1 and Cynthia Cooper at No. 2—has already lit up timelines. It’s a list destined to divide, because clutch is subjective. It’s about cold-blooded shots in big moments, but also about sustained pressure in playoff crucibles. And as the tweet pointed out, no fan is going to agree on every placement. Let’s break down the hottest points of contention.

The Maya Moore vs. Sheryl Swoopes Dilemma

Maya Moore at No. 3 over Sheryl Swoopes at No. 5 is a spicy take. Moore was a machine in the postseason, with two Finals MVPs and a habit of hitting game-winners in the Lynx’s dynasty run. She elevated her game when the lights were brightest. But Swoopes? She’s a three-time DPOY and four-time champion who delivered in the Comets’ four-peat era. Her clutch résumé includes iconic defensive stops and scoring bursts when Houston needed them most. Fans argue that Swoopes’ impact in the clutch—especially in an era with fewer offensive tools—should put her higher. The ranking seems to favor Moore’s shooting percentage in close games, but clutch isn’t just stats; it’s gravitas.

Diana Taurasi at No. 7 and the Recency Bias Question

Diana Taurasi at No. 7 feels low for a player nicknamed “White Mamba.” She has three rings and a reputation for dagger threes, but her ranking may reflect a dip in her recent play. Taurasi is still elite, but younger fans might not remember her 2009 playoff explosions. Still, her clutch moments—like her 47-point game in the 2009 Finals—are legendary. Placing her at 7 suggests recency bias is undervaluing her two-decade consistency. Conversely, some argue her shot selection in late-game situations has been less efficient lately, which could justify the drop.

Breanna Stewart at No. 10: The Missing Buzzer-Beater

Breanna Stewart at No. 10 is the ranking most likely to age well or poorly. Stewie has won everywhere—college, Olympics, overseas, and two WNBA titles—but she hasn’t had that singular, iconic buzzer-beater moment. She dominates through all-around excellence, not just last-second heroics. In her 2023 Finals MVP run, she hit clutch free throws and crucial shots, but they weren’t game-winners. Critics say her “clutch” status is more about consistency than one defining play. Give it time, and she’ll likely crack the top five.

Conclusion

No ranking of clutch players will satisfy everyone—that’s the beauty of the debate. Each name sparks a memory of a game-winning shot or a defensive stop. What’s clear is that the WNBA’s history is rich with pressure performers, and the arguments only grow as new stars like A’ja Wilson and Breanna Stewart write their own clutch chapters. So, which player do you think deserves a higher spot?
