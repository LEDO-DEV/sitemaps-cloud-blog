Pulisic sits but U.S. finds a way to reach KO roun...

United States · 2026-06-20

The U.S. Men’s National Team (USMNT) just sent a powerful message to the rest of the world: they are no longer a one-man show. With talisman Christian Pulisic sitting out due to caution management, the team secured a gritty 2-0 victory over Australia, advancing to the knockout rounds of the World Cup. For the first time since 1930, the USMNT has won back-to-back World Cup matches without their star captain on the field. This isn’t just a win—it’s a declaration of depth, resilience, and tactical maturity under manager Mauricio Pochettino.

Depth Shines as Pulisic Sits

The narrative leading into this match was dominated by one question: Can the USMNT win without Pulisic? The answer came early and emphatically. In just the 11th minute, Folarin Balogun tore through the Australian defense, leaving Alessandro Circati flat-footed before forcing an own goal from Cameron Burgess. It was a moment of pure individual brilliance, but it was also a testament to the system Pochettino has instilled. Even without their creative hub, the team pressed high, moved the ball quickly, and exploited spaces ruthlessly.

The second goal, scored by veteran Jordan Freeman in the 43rd minute, was scrappy and clinical. A loose ball in the box, a quick reaction, and the net bulged. No Pulisic. No drop-off. This is what depth looks like when every player on the roster understands their role. Pochettino’s system works with or without the star, and the clean sheet proved that the defense is just as reliable.

Context: A Historic Milestone and a Growing Belief

This match also carried broader significance. It marked the 1,000th official match for the U.S. men’s national team, a milestone that highlights how far the program has come from its humble beginnings. Japan’s second match at the World Cup also fell on this historic day, drawing attention to the global stage. But for American fans, the real story was witnessing a team that no longer depends solely on one player to deliver results.

“Nobody’s ready for this U.S. team in the knockout rounds,” one observer noted. And they might be right. With Balogun finding form, Freeman stepping up, and the defense looking organized, the USMNT has transformed into a collective threat. Pulisic sitting wasn’t a crisis—it was an opportunity for others to rise.

Conclusion: Dreaming Big Is Now Reality

It’s finally OK to dream big about the USMNT. This team has proven they can adapt, grind, and win without their best player. Against Australia, they didn’t just survive—they dominated. As they head into the knockout rounds, the message is clear: the U.S. is a complete team, not just a collection of individuals. And if they can maintain this focus, history might just be waiting to be rewritten.
