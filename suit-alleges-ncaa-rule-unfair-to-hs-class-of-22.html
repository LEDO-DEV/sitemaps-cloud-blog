Suit alleges NCAA rule unfair to HS Class of '22

United States · 2026-06-24

The COVID Class Hits a Wall: Why 15 Athletes Are Suing the NCAA

For the high school Class of 2022, college athletics was supposed to be a fresh start after a pandemic-ravaged junior and senior year. Instead, many feel the NCAA pulled the rug out from under them. A new lawsuit filed by 15 current and former athletes alleges that a recent eligibility rule change unfairly targets this specific group, commonly called the "COVID class." The plaintiffs argue the rule, which caps eligibility based on when a player turns 19, retroactively shortens the window for those who already lost a season of high school competition.

The core of the complaint centers on the NCAA’s new five-year eligibility clock, which starts counting from the date an athlete turns 19. For typical students, this isn’t a major shift. But for the Class of 2022, many were older when they graduated due to pandemic-related delays or enrollment choices. The suit claims these athletes are now competing against fifth-year seniors who benefited from pandemic waivers—players who already have an extra year of development. One player told reporters the rule feels like “the NCAA just gave the Class of 22 the middle finger,” summarizing the frustration. A judge has already denied a temporary restraining order, with a hearing set for next Wednesday.

The NCAA’s Defense and the Bigger Picture

In its official response, the NCAA argues that the rule is straightforward and fair: “You got your full eligibility.” The organization points out that these athletes have received the same five-year window as everyone else, even if they started later. The lawsuit suggests this ignores the unique context. The Class of 2022 didn’t just lose a season of high school competition—they entered a college landscape flooded with pandemic-waiver athletes who blocked playing time. Meanwhile, the NCAA is also considering broader changes to FBS practice and portal windows, making this legal battle even more consequential. If the plaintiffs succeed, it could force a re-evaluation of how eligibility rules apply to classes affected by national emergencies.

The hearing next Wednesday will be critical. If the court sides with the athletes, it could open the door for thousands of Class of 2022 players to request eligibility extensions. If not, it sets a precedent that the NCAA’s administrative rules can override the competitive disadvantages created by COVID-19. Either way, this lawsuit highlights a deeper question: how should eligibility policies account for the unique challenges faced by the COVID class? The answer won’t just affect these 15 players—it will shape the future of college sports for years to come.
