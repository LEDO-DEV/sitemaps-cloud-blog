‘Sumpa Kingdom’ Screens at Shanghai, Director Lhapal Gyal and Cast Discuss Sacred Mountain Landscapes

United States · 2026-06-20

The buzz around "Sumpa Kingdom" at the Shanghai International Film Festival (SIFF) is undeniable, but much of the conversation has fixated on its breathtaking sacred mountain landscapes. While these visuals are stunning, they risk overshadowing the film’s deeper, more daring core. Directed by Lhapal Gyal, this is not a simple travelogue. It is a quiet, radical statement about cultural ownership, hidden history, and the power of trusting a non-actor to carry a story about his own land.

The Radical Choice of a Real Tour Guide

The most talked-about behind-the-scenes detail is that Lhapal Gyal cast Tenzin Tsundue—a former tour guide with zero acting experience—as the lead. No audition. The director simply said, “I knew he was fully capable.” This decision is more than a gimmick; it is a deliberate act of authenticity. Tsundue’s presence grounds the film in a lived reality. His performance, described as understated and pure, fits the narrative’s characterizations far better than a trained actor’s could. In a cinema world obsessed with star power, Gyal’s trust in a local non-actor is a subtle but potent rebellion.

Sacred Mountains as the True Protagonist

Audiences keep praising the landscapes, but the conversation must shift to what makes "Sumpa Kingdom" dangerous. The sacred mountain at the story’s heart is not just scenery; it is the film’s protagonist. Lhapal Gyal uses it to explore suppressed histories and the spiritual connection between the Tibetan people and their land. The romance is secondary—a narrative device to highlight how modernity and tradition collide. When Gyal says the sacred mountain is the point, he challenges viewers to see beyond visual beauty and confront the erased stories that the film quietly resurrects. This unspoken tension is the film’s most radical layer.

A Quiet Statement at SIFF

If you dismiss "Sumpa Kingdom" as a pretty travelogue, you missed the entire conversation happening at SIFF. This film is proof that trusting the voice of a community—embodied by a first-time actor—can create something more powerful than any celebrity performance. The real danger of "Sumpa Kingdom" lies not in its plot but in its defiant refusal to compromise. Lhapal Gyal has made a film that dares you to look deeper. And that is precisely why it matters.
