If anyone can catch Wyndham Clark at the 2026 U.S....

United States · 2026-06-21

With a commanding six-shot lead heading into the final round of the 2026 U.S. Open, Wyndham Clark has put himself in a position that seems nearly unassailable. Even as Scottie Scheffler went three under par on the back nine during the third round, he still lost ground to Clark’s relentless pace. The question now is not just whether Clark can hold on, but if anyone can catch him—and what it would take to break the stranglehold he has placed on the championship.

The Size of the Gap

A six-shot lead at a U.S. Open is a rare and formidable advantage. Historically, players with such a cushion on the final day convert to victory more than 90% of the time. Wyndham Clark's play has been marked by precision off the tee and a steely short game that has neutralized the typically punishing rough and firm greens. Meanwhile, Scottie Scheffler, playing what would be a normal spectacular round for most, still saw the gap widen. This underscores how Clark has elevated his game to a level that negates even the world number one's best efforts.

For the chasing pack, the math is cruel. They need not only a career day—likely a round in the low 60s—but also a collapse from Clark. At the U.S. Open, where the leader often backs into a win with pars, Clark is playing aggressively, making birdies when others are scrambling for bogey. The back nine at this venue offers few scoring opportunities for charging players, especially with the pressure of a major championship on the line.

Can the Field Mount a Comeback?

History offers some hope, but not much. The largest final-round comeback in U.S. Open history is five shots, and that required a combination of a leader faltering and a challenger producing a historic round. While names like Scottie Scheffler, who has a knack for late charges, and other contenders like Xander Schauffele or Rory McIlroy could theoretically heat up, the margin is simply too wide for them to solely rely on their own play.

The ultimate factor may be Wyndham Clark himself. If he starts the round with a few routine pars, the pressure shifts entirely to the chasers. The course setup for the final round—likely with tucked pins and firmer fairways—will reward Clark’s steady hand. As the gallery roars for a charge, Clark’s biggest test will be mental: sticking to his game plan and ignoring the possibility of history slipping away. Right now, the odds heavily favor Clark raising the trophy, leaving his rivals to wonder what might have been.
